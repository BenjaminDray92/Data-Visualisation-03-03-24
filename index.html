Visualization 1

This graph juxtaposes the predicted values of the S&P 500 (dotted blue line) with the actual values (green line), allowing for a direct comparison of the prediction model's performance. The addition of a histogram of residuals (red bars) on a secondary axis provides an instant view of the discrepancies between predictions and actual values. Significant residuals indicate where the model is less accurate, providing insights into the performance and reliability of the model across different time intervals.
The scatter plot displays the residuals (the difference between predicted and actual values) on the vertical axis against dates on the horizontal axis. The residuals are colored green for positive values and red for negative values, allowing for a quick visual distinction between model underestimations and overestimations. A trendline is also added to show the average evolution of the residuals over a 30-day moving window, providing a visual indication of any persistent trend in the residuals over time.
Adjacent to the scatter plot, the residual histogram reveals the distribution of discrepancies between predicted and actual values.

For each asset, an ACF (AutoCorrelation Function) plot is drawn, illustrating how current values of the asset are correlated with its past values up to 40 time lags. These plots show the strength and duration of autocorrelation for each asset.
The PACF (Partial AutoCorrelation Function) plots show the partial autocorrelation for each asset, meaning the autocorrelation for a specific number of lags, while removing the influence of intermediate lags. They are essential for identifying the direct relationship between current values and their past values at different time intervals. The biplot of Principal Component Analysis (PCA) illustrates the dimensional reduction of financial data into two principal components, PC1 and PC2.
PC1, the horizontal axis, captures the largest share of variance in the data, while PC2, the vertical axis, captures the second largest share. The blue points represent individual observations in this reduced space, where proximity suggests similar performance characteristics among assets.
The red arrows indicate the influence and direction of the original variables. The length of each arrow denotes the strength of the variable's impact on the components, with longer arrows indicating greater influence. The orientation of the arrows demonstrates the correlation between variables: arrows pointing in similar directions indicate positive correlation, while arrows in opposite directions reveal negative correlation.
This dynamic plot explores the evolving correlation between different financial indices and gold over a specified period. It utilizes bubbles whose size varies based on the magnitude of the correlation, providing a visual representation of how the relationship between these assets and gold changes over time. A larger bubble indicates a stronger correlation.
This graph illustrates the volatility of returns for various indices and financial assets, as modeled by ARCH (Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity) models. It shows both the logarithmic returns and conditional volatility for each instrument.

Discussion


An important aspect of this work has been data management, including the removal of NaN values, to ensure the reliability and accuracy of analyses.
Each visualization, whether comparing predictions to actual values, examining dynamic correlations, visualizing volatility, or analyzing residuals, contributes to a better understanding of trends and behaviors in the financial market.
Creating these visualizations involved challenges such as presenting complex information in a clear and intuitive manner, using effective visual design and distinct colors to facilitate interpretation.
